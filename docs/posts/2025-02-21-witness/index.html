There is so much happening all around us right now, a deluge of panic and uncertainty, bad news and fear, suffering and injustice. It’s easy to feel overwhelmed and to easily slip into familiar habits. Some of us retreat– we stop watching or reading or listening to the news and focus only on what’s in front of us. Some of us jump into action– organizing, contacting, checking up, adding new meetings to our schedules and to-dos to our list. Some of us spiral– should we start stocking up on canned food? Is now the time to look into residency requirements elsewhere. Some of us ping-pong between all three, hunkering down one day, over-committing the next, only to doom-spiral out at 1 a.m. watching videos about the perfect go-bag.

As we come to the end of this fourth week, I’ve found myself thinking often of pacing. We’re only about a month in– how will we ensure that we move at a pace we can sustain? How many of us, in the words of Anne Helen Peterson, are feeling this in our bodies right now? How can we create space to sit with the reality of this moment, to “see things as they really are,” and not forget the vital work of witness?

In times like these, I tend towards either action of avoidance. You will find me either completely offline or deep in the weeds of meetings and phone calls and cooking and organizing and whatever other task I’ve found that seems useful in the moment. Both of these reactions allow me to sidestep my complex and potentially overwhelming feelings, to stuff grief and hurt and anger and fear down for another day, to be dealt with in an undefined ‘later’ time. I avoid and act and avoid and act until finally the dam of feelings is breached, perhaps by a fight with my spouse, bad news from a friend, a task gone wrong and work– and suddenly I am drowning. Or perhaps these feelings transmute themselves into a spasming back or a sharp twinge in my right hip as my body pulls the brake on constant motion. Either way, we cannot avoid these feelings forever, through inaction or constant doing, distraction or determination.

So instead, let’s make room for the work of witness.

The work of witness asks us to stop for a moment– stop doing, stop avoiding, and be still. Look, listen, feel. Take in our current moment. See who is being harmed and who is determining what harm is done, who has power and who does not, who is speaking and who is silent, who is valued and who is discarded. Sit with the feelings that arise from your observation of the situation– anger, sadness, fear, joy, hope, terror, despair, grief, rage, pity, pride, disgust– as each feeling comes, greet it as a guest. Call it by name, and see what it has to tell you. Try as best you can not to judge the feelings or to jump to action, but rather to be present with whatever comes. Then, after some time, move to thinking about what you might do now. Perhaps you want to share what you felt with a friend or write it down. Maybe you want to call your congressperson and tell them what you are feeling. Perhaps there is something you can do to support those most in harm’s way. May we see and feel what is happening all around us and take the time to respect the weight of it. Then may we, together, choose to move towards caring for one another.
